# NDVI / Vegetation Indices: MODIS and VIIRS

*Last updated: March 2025*

## Overview

The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) is one of the most widely used remotely sensed indicators in earth science. It quantifies vegetation greenness and photosynthetic activity by exploiting the contrast between red (absorbed by chlorophyll) and near-infrared (reflected by leaf structure) reflectance:

```
NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red)
```

NDVI ranges from -1 to +1. Values above 0.3 generally indicate healthy vegetation; values near 0 indicate bare soil or water; negative values indicate water, snow, or clouds.

For the Causal Atlas project, NDVI serves as a critical environmental variable linking climate to food security, agricultural productivity, and downstream socioeconomic outcomes. Three primary satellite product families provide global NDVI time series:

1. **MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer)** -- Terra (launched 1999) and Aqua (launched 2002) satellites. The longest continuous moderate-resolution NDVI record (February 2000--present). Multiple spatial resolutions from 250 m to 0.05 degrees.

2. **VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite)** -- Suomi-NPP (2012--present) and NOAA-20 (2018--present). The operational successor to MODIS with improved calibration and spatial resolution.

3. **AVHRR (Advanced Very High Resolution Radiometer)** -- Legacy sensors providing NDVI from 1981, but with significant quality limitations. Not recommended as primary source but useful for long historical context.

| Value | Meaning | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Good data, use with confidence | Include |
| 1 | Marginal data, useful but look at detailed QA | Include with caution |

#### MOD13C2 (Monthly, 0.05 degree CMG) -- Alternative for Global Grid

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Short name** | MOD13C2 |
| **Version** | 6.1 (061) |
| **DOI** | `https://doi.org/10.5067/MODIS/MOD13C2.061` |
| **Spatial resolution** | 0.05 degrees (~5.6 km) |
| **Grid** | Geographic lat/lon Climate Modeling Grid (CMG): 3600 rows x 7200 columns |
| **Temporal coverage** | February 2000 -- present |

The CMG product includes the same vegetation indices plus spatial statistics (mean, standard deviation, number of input pixels used) which are useful for quality assessment at coarser resolution.

**Key advantage for Causal Atlas**: Already in geographic lat/lon projection, so no sinusoidal reprojection needed. At 0.05 degrees, roughly 10 CMG pixels fit within each 0.5-degree PRIO-GRID cell (10x10 = 100 pixels), enabling straightforward aggregation.

## Coverage

### Spatial Coverage

All products provide **global land coverage** (ocean pixels are masked or set to fill values).

- MODIS sinusoidal tiles cover the globe in a grid of ~460 tiles (only ~326 contain land)
- CMG products are single global files
- VIIRS uses the same sinusoidal tiling scheme as MODIS

### Temporal Coverage Summary

| Product Family | Start | End | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| MODIS Terra (MOD13) | Feb 2000 | Ongoing (~2026+) | 25+ years |
| MODIS Aqua (MYD13) | Jul 2002 | Ongoing | 23+ years |
| VIIRS NPP (VNP13) | Jan 2012 | Ongoing | 13+ years |

**MODIS end-of-life note**: Terra has been in a descending orbit since 2020 and data quality is degrading. Aqua's orbit is also degrading. VIIRS on SNPP and NOAA-20 provides continuity. NASA maintains cross-calibration between MODIS and VIIRS to ensure continuity of the vegetation index record.

## Quality Considerations

### Cloud Contamination

Cloud cover is the primary quality issue for NDVI products. The 16-day compositing window uses a constrained view angle, maximum value composite (CV-MVC) algorithm to select the best available pixel, but persistent cloud cover (e.g., tropical wet seasons, monsoon regions) can still contaminate composites.

**Mitigation strategies**:
1. Filter by `SummaryQA` or `pixel_reliability` to exclude cloudy pixels
2. Use monthly composites (MOD13A3/MYD13A3) which average over more observations
3. Apply temporal gap-filling (e.g., Savitzky-Golay smoothing, harmonic regression)
4. Combine Terra and Aqua for higher observation frequency

### Atmospheric Effects

MODIS C61 vegetation indices apply atmospheric correction using surface reflectance products. However, heavy aerosol loads (Saharan dust, biomass burning smoke) can still affect NDVI quality. The `DetailedQA` aerosol quantity bits indicate aerosol contamination level.

### Snow and Ice

Snow-covered pixels produce low or negative NDVI values and must be filtered using the `SummaryQA` snow/ice flag (value=2) or the `DetailedQA` snow/ice bit. In high-latitude and mountainous regions, this reduces the effective growing season length of usable data.

### Bidirectional Reflectance Effects

Sun-sensor geometry affects reflectance values and can introduce systematic biases, particularly at high latitudes and at the edges of the satellite swath. The MODIS VI algorithm applies BRDF correction where possible (indicated by DetailedQA bit 9).

### MODIS Sensor Degradation

- **Terra**: Launched December 1999. The sensor has been operational for 25+ years, well beyond its 6-year design life. Orbit is degrading, causing shifting equatorial crossing times that affect illumination conditions and data quality.
- **Aqua**: Launched May 2002. Similar aging concerns.
- **Impact**: Long-term trend analysis using MODIS should account for potential sensor degradation artefacts, especially post-2020 for Terra.

### MODIS-VIIRS Continuity

NASA has invested significantly in cross-calibrating MODIS and VIIRS vegetation index products to ensure a seamless long-term record. The VIIRS VNP13 products are designed to be scientifically compatible with MOD13 products. However, residual differences exist due to different spectral response functions and view geometries. For trend analysis spanning the MODIS-VIIRS transition, validation against ground truth is recommended.

## Python Access

### Google Earth Engine (via `ee` Python API)

```python
import ee
ee.Authenticate()
ee.Initialize()

# Monthly MODIS NDVI (1 km)
ndvi_collection = ee.ImageCollection('MODIS/061/MOD13A3') \
    .filterDate('2020-01-01', '2020-12-31') \
    .select('NDVI')

# Apply scale factor
def scale_ndvi(image):
    return image.multiply(0.0001).copyProperties(image, ['system:time_start'])

ndvi_scaled = ndvi_collection.map(scale_ndvi)

# Compute annual mean NDVI
annual_mean = ndvi_scaled.mean()

# Extract for a PRIO-GRID cell (0.5 x 0.5 degrees)
cell = ee.Geometry.Rectangle([36.0, -1.0, 36.5, -0.5])
stats = annual_mean.reduceRegion(
    reducer=ee.Reducer.mean(),
    geometry=cell,
    scale=1000
)
print(stats.getInfo())  # {'NDVI': 0.xxxx}
```

### Quality-Filtered NDVI Time Series (GEE)

```python
import ee
ee.Initialize()

# Filter to good quality pixels only
def mask_quality(image):
    qa = image.select('SummaryQA')
    good = qa.lte(1)  # 0=Good, 1=Marginal
    return image.select('NDVI').multiply(0.0001).updateMask(good) \
        .copyProperties(image, ['system:time_start'])

ndvi = ee.ImageCollection('MODIS/061/MOD13A3') \
    .filterDate('2015-01-01', '2023-12-31') \
    .map(mask_quality)

# Compute monthly mean for a region
region = ee.Geometry.Rectangle([30, -5, 35, 0])  # Burundi/Rwanda region

def extract_monthly(image):
    date = ee.Date(image.get('system:time_start'))
    mean = image.reduceRegion(
        reducer=ee.Reducer.mean(),
        geometry=region,
        scale=1000
    )
    return ee.Feature(None, {
        'date': date.format('YYYY-MM'),
        'ndvi': mean.get('NDVI')
    })

ts = ndvi.map(extract_monthly)
print(ts.getInfo())
```

### NDVI Anomaly Calculation

```python
import xarray as xr
import numpy as np

# Assuming monthly NDVI as xarray Dataset with dimensions (time, lat, lon)
ds = xr.open_mfdataset("ndvi_monthly_*.nc")

# Compute climatological mean and standard deviation (2001-2020 baseline)
baseline = ds.sel(time=slice("2001-01-01", "2020-12-31"))
clim_mean = baseline.groupby("time.month").mean("time")
clim_std = baseline.groupby("time.month").std("time")

# Compute z-score anomalies
anomaly = (ds.groupby("time.month") - clim_mean) / clim_std

# Flag severe negative anomalies (drought indicator)
drought_flag = anomaly < -1.5  # 1.5 standard deviations below normal
```

## Relevance to Causal Atlas

### Primary Use Cases

1. **Drought monitoring and early warning**: NDVI anomalies (z-scores below long-term mean) are the standard satellite-based indicator for agricultural drought. Below-average NDVI during the growing season is predictive of crop failure and food insecurity with 1-3 month lead time.

2. **Food security causal chains**: The chain rainfall (CHIRPS) -> vegetation response (NDVI) -> crop production (FAO) -> food prices (WFP) -> food insecurity (IPC/CH) is one of the most well-established cross-domain causal relationships. NDVI is the critical intermediate variable.

3. **Climate-conflict nexus**: Published research examines NDVI anomalies as a factor in pastoral and agricultural conflict, particularly in the Sahel, Horn of Africa, and South Asia. Time-lagged relationships between vegetation stress and conflict incidence can be tested using Granger causality and related methods.

4. **Vegetation change as context variable**: Long-term NDVI trends reveal desertification, deforestation, and land degradation -- all of which provide context for interpreting conflict and food security dynamics.

5. **Disaster validation**: Drought events recorded in EM-DAT can be validated and spatially refined using NDVI anomaly maps. Flood impacts on agriculture are also visible as anomalous NDVI drops during the growing season.

### Recommended Products for Causal Atlas

| Use Case | Recommended Product | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly time series (2000--present) | **MOD13A3** (1 km monthly) | Longest continuous record, monthly cadence matches PRIO-GRID and most other datasets |
| Global grid analysis | **MOD13C2** (0.05 deg monthly CMG) | Already in geographic projection, easy aggregation to PRIO-GRID |
| Higher temporal resolution | **MOD13A2** (1 km, 16-day) | For sub-monthly lag analysis |
| VIIRS-era analysis (2012+) | **VNP13A1** (500 m, 16-day) | Higher resolution, continuity beyond MODIS |
| Fine-scale agriculture | **MOD13Q1** (250 m, 16-day) | For sub-national agricultural analysis |

### Integration Strategy

1. **Primary**: Use MOD13A3 monthly NDVI via Google Earth Engine for server-side aggregation to 0.5-degree PRIO-GRID cells.
2. **Quality filtering**: Apply SummaryQA mask (retain values 0 and 1) before aggregation.
3. **Anomaly calculation**: Compute z-score anomalies against 2001-2020 baseline climatology.
4. **Storage**: Monthly NDVI mean, anomaly, and observation count per grid cell in Parquet format.
5. **Lag analysis**: Pre-compute NDVI at lags of 1, 2, 3, 6 months for cross-correlation with conflict, food price, and other variables.
6. **Continuity**: Plan for MODIS end-of-life by establishing VIIRS-MODIS cross-calibration for the overlap period (2012--present).

### Key Research on NDVI and Causal Chains

- Vegetation stress is predictive of below-average crop yields up to 3 months in advance (multiple studies using FEWS NET methodology)
- NDVI anomalies in the Sahel are significantly correlated with subsequent pastoral conflict with 1-2 season lags
- Combined NDVI and rainfall anomalies improve food price spike prediction over either variable alone
- NDVI-derived vegetation health index (VHI) has been used to estimate crop yield losses of up to 39% for rice, 34% for sugarcane, and 25% for wheat during drought years
